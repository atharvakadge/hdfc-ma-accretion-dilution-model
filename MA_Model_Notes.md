# M&A Accretion/Dilution — Interview Notes
### Project: HDFC Bank acquires HDFC Ltd. (all-stock, ~$40B, July 2023)

---

## 1. THE CORE QUESTION
Does the **combined company's EPS** go UP (accretive) or DOWN (dilutive) vs the acquirer's standalone EPS?

**EPS = Net Income ÷ Shares Outstanding**

In a merger BOTH change:
- Numerator ↑ (add target's net income + synergies)
- Denominator ↑ (issue new shares to pay for target)

> Accretive if **% rise in Net Income > % rise in Share Count**

---

## 2. THE GOLDEN RULE (all-stock deals only)
| Condition | Result |
|-----------|--------|
| Acquirer P/E > Target P/E | **Accretive** |
| Acquirer P/E < Target P/E | **Dilutive** |
| Acquirer P/E = Target P/E | **Neutral** |

**Why:** P/E = price paid per ₹1 of earnings. Buying earnings *cheaper* than your own stock adds to EPS.
- High acquirer P/E = "expensive currency" → buy cheap earnings → accretive
- Low acquirer P/E = "cheap currency" → must issue more shares → dilutive

**CAVEAT:** Rule only holds for all-stock deals. Add cash/debt → compare target earnings yield vs after-tax cost of financing instead.

---

## 3. THE HDFC DEAL — KEY FACTS
- **Reverse merger:** subsidiary (HDFC Bank) absorbed parent (HDFC Ltd)
- **Swap ratio:** 42 HDFC Bank shares per 25 HDFC Ltd shares = **1.68x**
- **New shares issued:** ~311 crore
- **100% stock deal** (no cash)
- Both at ~20x P/E → deal was **EPS-neutral (~+0.8%)**

---

## 4. WHY DO A NEUTRAL DEAL? (strategic rationale)
EPS wasn't the point. Real drivers:
- **Regulatory** — NBFC rules tightening; becoming a bank solved it
- **Cross-sell** — only ~2% of HDFC Ltd mortgage customers used HDFC Bank products
- **Cheap funding** — bank deposits cheaper than NBFC borrowing
- **Scale** — created India's 2nd largest bank, 4th most valuable globally
- **Synergies realize over years**, not day-1 EPS

---

## 5. MODEL STRUCTURE (3 sheets)
1. **Acquirer** — NI, Shares, EPS, Price, P/E (standalone)
2. **Target** — same structure
3. **Merger Model:**
   - Standalone (linked from both sheets)
   - Deal terms: swap ratio → new shares = target shares × swap ratio
   - Combined: NI = A + B + synergies; Shares = A's shares + new issued
   - Result: Accretion = Combined EPS − Standalone EPS

---

## 6. CRITICAL CONCEPTS TO NAIL

**Standalone vs Consolidated** (my model trap & fix)
- Must compare like-for-like. Standalone NI excludes subsidiary profits but market price reflects them → distorts P/E.
- Fix: used **consolidated** figures for both → realistic P/Es (~20x each).

**Accretion ≠ Good Deal**
- Accretive but value-destroying: bought cheap earnings with overvalued stock, bad business
- Dilutive but value-creating: paid up for high-growth target that compounds (most tech M&A)
- Real test: **ROIC vs Cost of Capital**, strategic fit, synergy realization, price vs intrinsic value

**Synergies**
- Cost synergies (easier, more credible) + Revenue synergies (harder to realize)
- Adding synergies to combined NI → pushes deal more accretive

---

## 7. LIKELY INTERVIEW QUESTIONS
**Q: Walk me through an accretion/dilution model.**
→ Standalone EPS of each → deal terms (swap ratio, new shares) → combine NI and shares → new EPS → compare.

**Q: All-stock deal, acquirer P/E lower than target. Accretive or dilutive?**
→ Dilutive.

**Q: How do you make a dilutive deal accretive?**
→ More cash/debt financing (if cheap), higher synergies, lower premium paid, buybacks.

**Q: Is accretion always good?**
→ No. Value creation matters more. Check ROIC vs WACC.

**Q: Cash vs stock deal — which is more accretive?**
→ Usually cash/debt (if interest cost after-tax < target earnings yield), since no share dilution. But adds leverage/risk.
